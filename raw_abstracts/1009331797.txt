In sociology and psychology, mass hysteria (also known as mass psychogenic illness, collective hysteria, group hysteria, or collective obsessional behavior) is a phenomenon that transmits collective illusions of threats, whether real or imaginary, through a population and society as a result of rumors and fear.<ref>Wolf, M. (1976). Witchcraft and Mass Hysteria in Terms of Current Psychological Theories, (are caused by the use of medical/experimental delusions). Journal of Practical Nursing and Mental Health Services 14: 23–28.</ref><ref name="Bartholomew 2001">Bartholomew, Robert E. (2001). Little Green Men, Meowing Nuns and Head-Hunting Panics: A Study of Mass Psychogenic Illness and Social Delusion. McFarland & Company.</ref>
In medicine, the term is used to describe the spontaneous manifestation—or production of chemicals in the body—of the same or similar hysterical physical symptoms by more than one person.<ref name="bartholomew-wessely"></ref><ref name="waller-guardian"></ref>
A common type of mass hysteria occurs when a group of people believes that it is suffering from a similar disease or ailment,<ref name="csicop.org"></ref> sometimes referred to as mass psychogenic illness or epidemic hysteria.<ref name="Weir">Mass, Weir E.
