The term assault weapon is used in the United States to define some types of firearms.<ref name=Goode130116/> The definition varies among regulating jurisdictions but usually includes semi-automatic firearms chambered for centerfire ammunition with a detachable magazine, a pistol grip and sometimes other features such as a vertical forward grip, flash suppressor or barrel shroud.<ref name=Goode130116/><ref name=Babay121222/> Some firearms are specified by name.<ref name=Levs130131/> At the time that the now-defunct Federal Assault Weapons Ban passed in 1994, the U.S. Department of Justice said, "In general, assault weapons are semiautomatic firearms with a large magazine of ammunition that were designed and configured for rapid fire and combat use."<ref name=Levs130131/> The origin of the term has been attributed to legislators, gun control groups, firearm manufacturers, and the media.<ref name=Goode130116/><ref name=Tartaro1995/><ref name=Blake130117/><ref name=Kauffman121218/><ref name=GDBG2AWs2008/> It is often used interchangeably (but incorrectly) with the term "assault rifle", which may more specifically refer to selective-fire military rifles that can fire in automatic or burst mode.<ref name=Blake130117/>
After the December 2012 Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting, many news organizations ran stories about assault weapons, explaining their varying definitions and presenting varying opinions about whether they should be banned again at the federal level.<ref name=Goode130116></ref><ref name=Blake130117></ref><ref name=Lallanilla130117></ref><ref name=Richman130118/>
