Foucault's Pendulum (original title: Il pendolo di Foucault is a novel by Italy writer and philosopher Umberto Eco. It was first published in 1988, and an English translation by William Weaver appeared a year later.<ref>Barber, Malcolm. "The Trial of the Templars" Second Edition. .Cambridge University Press, 2006. . Page viii</ref>
Foucault's Pendulum is divided into ten segments represented by the ten Sephirot. The satirical novel is full of esoteric references to Kabbalah, alchemy, and conspiracy theory—so many that critic and novelist Anthony Burgess suggested that it needed an index.<ref></ref> The pendulum of the title refers to Foucault pendulum designed by French physicist Léon Foucault to demonstrate Earth's rotation, which has symbolic significance within the novel. Some believe that it refers to Michel Foucault,<ref>The novel ends the day after Michel Foucault died (25th June 1984).</ref> noting Eco's friendship with the French philosopher,<ref>D. Defert, "Chronologie", in M. Foucault, Dits et écrits, Gallimard, Paris 1994 (2001), p. 41.</ref> but the author "specifically rejects any intentional reference to Michel Foucault"<ref>"Umberto Eco & The Open Text" by Peter E. Bondanella p. 133</ref>—this is regarded as one of his subtle literary jokes.<ref></ref>
