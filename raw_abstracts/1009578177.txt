In political science, a reactionary or reactionist is a person or entity holding political views that favour a return to a wikt:status quo ante of society that they believe possessed positive characteristics that are absent in contemporary society. As an adjective, the word reactionary describes points of view and policies meant to restore a past status quo.<ref>The New Fontaena Dictionary of Modern Thought Third Edition, (1999) p. 729.</ref> The word reactionary is often used in the context of the left–right political spectrum, and is one tradition in right-wing politics. In popular usage, it is commonly used to refer to a highly traditional position, one opposed to social or political change.<ref></ref><ref></ref> However, according to political theorist Mark Lilla, a reactionary yearns to overturn a present condition of perceived decadence and recover an idealized past. Such reactionary individuals and policies favour social transformation, in contrast to Conservatism individuals or policies that seek incremental change or to preserve what exists in the present.<ref></ref>
Reactionary ideologies can be radical, in the sense of Extremism, in service to re-establishing past conditions. In political discourse, being a reactionary is generally regarded as negative; Peter King observed that it is "an unsought-for label, used as a torment rather than a badge of honour."<ref>King, Peter. Reaction: Against the modern world. Andrews UK Limited, 2012.</ref> Despite this, the descriptor "political reactionary" has been adopted by writers such as the Austrian monarchist Erik von Kuehnelt-Leddihn,<ref> urltoken Credo of a Reactionary by Erik von Kuehnelt-Leddihn – The American Mercury, under his alias Francis Stuart Campbell</ref> the Scottish journalist Gerald Warner of Craigenmaddie,<ref></ref> the Colombian political theology Nicolás Gómez Dávila, and the American historian John Lukacs.<ref></ref>
