Ideas of reference and delusions of reference describe the phenomenon of an individual experiencing innocuous events or mere coincidences<ref></ref> and believing they have strong personal significance.<ref></ref> It is "the notion that everything one perceives in the world relates to one's own destiny", usually in a negative and hostile manner.<ref>Laurence M. Porter, Women's Vision in Western Literature (2005) p. 117</ref>
In psychiatry, delusions of reference form part of the diagnostic criteria for psychotic illnesses such as schizophrenia,<ref>Andreasen, Nancy C. (1984). urltoken "Scale for the assessment of positive symptoms" ; The Movement Disorder Society.</ref> delusional disorder, bipolar disorder (during the elevated stages of mania), as well as schizotypal personality disorder, <ref></ref> and even autism when under periods of intense stress.<ref></ref> To a lesser extent, it can be a hallmark of paranoid personality disorder, as well as body dysmorphic disorder. Such symptoms can also be caused by intoxication, such as stimulants like methamphetamine.
