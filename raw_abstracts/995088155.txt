Ephedra is a medicinal preparation from the plant Ephedra sinica.<ref name=Gurley_1998></ref> Several additional species belonging to the genus Ephedra have traditionally been used for a variety of medicinal purposes, and are a possible candidate for the Soma plant of Indo-Iranian religion.<ref> urltoken Botany of Haoma , from Encyclop√¶dia Iranica. Accessed March 15, 2007.</ref> It has been used in traditional Chinese medicine for more than 2,000 years.<ref name=Abourashed_2003></ref><ref name="Huang2010"></ref> Native Americans and Mormon pioneers drank a tea brewed from other Ephedra species, called "Mormon tea" and "Indian tea".
Image:Mormon Tea in Arches NP - July 2008.jpg|thumb|200px|right|Mormon tea (Ephedra funerea) growing in the wild in the Fiery Furnace area of Arches National Park near Moab, Utah
Dietary supplements containing ephedra alkaloids have been found to be unsafe, with reports of serious side effects and ephedra-related deaths.<ref name=Haller_2000></ref><ref name=Bent_2003></ref><ref name="nccih"></ref><ref name="fdamain"></ref> In response to accumulating evidence of adverse effects and deaths related to ephedra, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) banned the sale of supplements containing ephedrine alkaloids in 2004.<ref name="fdaban"></ref> The ban was challenged in court by ephedra manufacturers, but ultimately upheld in 2006 by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit.<ref name="fdaappeal"></ref> Ephedra extracts not containing ephedrine have not been banned by the FDA and are still sold legally.<ref name=":0"></ref>
