A credit union, a type of financial institution similar to a commercial bank, is a member-owned financial cooperative, controlled by its members and operated on a not-for-profit basis. Credit unions generally provide services to members similar to retail banks, including deposit accounts, provision of credit, and other financial services.<ref name="Credit Union Act 2007" /><ref name="O'Sullivan" />
Worldwide, credit union systems vary significantly in terms of total assets and average institution asset size, ranging from volunteer operations with a handful of members to institutions with hundreds of thousands of members and assets worth billions of US dollars.<ref name="cuna" /> In 2018 the number of members in credit unions worldwide was 274 million, with nearly 40 million members being added since 2016.<ref></ref>
Leading up to the financial crisis of 2007–2008, commercial banks engaged in approximately five times more subprime lending relative to credit unions and were two and a half times more likely to fail during the crisis.<ref></ref> American credit unions more than doubled lending to small businesses between 2008 and 2016, from $30 billion to $60 billion, while lending to small businesses overall during the same period declined by around $100 billion.<ref></ref> In the US, public trust in credit unions stands at 60%, compared to 30% for big banks.<ref></ref> Furthermore, small businesses are 80% less likely to be dissatisfied with a credit union than with a big bank.<ref> urltoken
