Ciclosporin, also spelled cyclosporine and cyclosporin, is a calcineurin inhibitor, used as an immunosuppressant medication. It is a natural product.<ref name=WHO2008></ref> It is taken by mouth or by intravenous for rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis, Crohn's disease, nephrotic syndrome, and in organ transplants to prevent rejection.<ref name=WHO2008/><ref name=AHFS2016></ref> It is also used as eye drops for keratoconjunctivitis sicca (dry eyes).<ref name=AHFS2016B></ref>
Common side effects include high blood pressure, headache, kidney problems, increased hair growth, and vomiting.<ref name=AHFS2016/> Other severe side effects include an increased risk of infection, liver problems, and an increased risk of lymphoma.<ref name=AHFS2016/> Blood levels of the medication should be checked to decrease the risk of side effects.<ref name=AHFS2016/> Use during pregnancy may result in preterm birth; however, ciclosporin does not appear to cause birth defects.<ref></ref>
Ciclosporin is believed to work by decreasing the function of lymphocytes.<ref name=AHFS2016/> It does this by forming a complex with cyclophilin to block the phosphatase activity of calcineurin, which in turn decreases the production of inflammatory cytokines by T cell.<ref name="Matsuda_2000" />
Ciclosporin was isolated in 1971 from the fungus Tolypocladium inflatum and came into medical use in 1983.<ref></ref> It is on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines, the safest and most effective medicines needed in a health system.<ref name="WHO21st"></ref> In 2017, it was the 248th most commonly prescribed medication in the United States, with more than one million prescriptions that year.<ref></ref><ref></ref>
