Pork barrel is a metaphor for the appropriation of government spending for localized projects secured solely or primarily to bring money to a representative's district. The usage originated in American English.<ref></ref> Scholars use it as a technical term regarding legislative control of local appropriations.<ref></ref><ref></ref> In election campaigns, the term is used in derogatory fashion to attack opponents. One of the most explicit definitions of the “pork barrel” is given by Sharma (2017):<ref></ref> “The term “pork‐barrel politics” refers to instances in which ruling parties channel public money to particular constituencies based on political considerations, at the expense of broader public interests”.
Typically, "pork" involves funding for government programs whose Economy or service benefits are concentrated in a particular area but whose costs are spread among all taxpayers. Public works projects, certain national defense spending projects, and Agricultural subsidy are the most commonly cited examples.
Citizens Against Government Waste<ref></ref> outlines seven criteria by which spending in the United States can be classified as "pork":
# Requested by only one chamber of Congress
# Not specifically authorized
# Not competitively awarded
# Not requested by the President
# Greatly exceeds the President's budget request or the previous year's funding
# Not the subject of Congressional hearings
# Serves only a local or special interest.
