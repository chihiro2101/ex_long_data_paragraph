Genital warts are a sexually transmitted infection caused by certain types of human papillomavirus (HPV).<ref name=NHS2017></ref> They are generally pink in color and project out from the surface of the skin.<ref name=Fer2018></ref> Usually they cause few symptoms, but can occasionally be painful.<ref name=CDC2010/> Typically they appear one to eight months following exposure.<ref name=AFP2010/> Warts are the most easily recognized symptom of genital HPV infection.<ref name=AFP2010/>
HPV types 6 and 11 are the typical cause of genital warts.<ref name=CDC2010></ref> It is spread through direct skin-to-skin contact, usually during oral, genital, or anal sex with an infected partner.<ref name=AFP2010/> Diagnosis is generally based on symptoms and can be confirmed by biopsy.<ref name=CDC2010/> The types of HPV that cause cancer are not the same as those that cause warts.<ref name=NCI2017></ref> 
 
Some HPV vaccines can prevent genital warts as may condoms.<ref name=AFP2010/><ref name=NHS2017/> Treatment options include creams such as podophyllin, imiquimod, and trichloroacetic acid.<ref name=CDC2010/> Cryotherapy or surgery may also be an option.<ref name=CDC2010/> After treatment warts often resolve within six months.<ref name=AFP2010/> Without treatment, in up to a third of cases they resolve on their own.<ref name=AFP2010/>
About 1% of people in the United States have genital warts.<ref name=AFP2010></ref> Many people, however, are infected and do not have symptoms.<ref name=AFP2010/> Without vaccination nearly all sexually active people will get some type of HPV at one point in their lives.<ref name=NCI2017/><ref name=CDC2017Fact></ref> The disease has been known at least since the time of Hippocrates in 300 BC.<ref></ref>
